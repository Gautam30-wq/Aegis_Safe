Societal Issues Women Face

Explore critical categories of challenges that affect women and girls worldwide. Expand each topic to learn about the scale, causes, and impact.

Gender Inequality

Systemic disparities in wages, career advancement, and political representation continue to limit women's full participation in the economy.

1 The Wage Gap

Globally, women earn approximately 77 cents for every dollar men earn.

  • The gap persists across all industries and education levels.
  • Women of color face compounded pay gaps (e.g., Latina women earn roughly 57 cents).
  • Factors include occupational segregation and lack of salary transparency.
2 Career Barriers

Women hold less than 30% of senior management positions globally.

  • The 'motherhood penalty' affects wages and promotion opportunities.
  • Lack of mentorship is a major hurdle in tech and finance sectors.
3 Leadership Representation

Women make up only 26% of national parliamentarians worldwide.

  • Only 26 countries currently have a woman Head of State.
  • Corporate boards remain heavily male-dominated at roughly 77%.
⚠

Violence Against Women

Physical, sexual, and psychological violence remains one of the most widespread human rights violations globally.

1 Domestic Violence

1 in 3 women worldwide have experienced intimate partner violence.

  • Lockdowns during the pandemic created a 'shadow pandemic' of abuse.
  • Economic dependence often traps victims with their abusers.
2 Sexual Harassment

736 million women have been subjected to sexual violence in their lifetime.

  • The #MeToo movement highlighted the systemic nature of workplace harassment.
  • Digital threats and cyberstalking are rapidly rising concerns.
3 Human Trafficking

Women and girls account for 71% of all trafficking victims.

  • Sexual exploitation is the primary form of trafficking globally.
  • Root causes include poverty, conflict, and lack of education.

Health & Wellbeing

Access to quality healthcare and education remains a fundamental barrier to gender equality and autonomy.

1 The Literacy Gap

Two-thirds of the world's 773 million illiterate adults are women.

  • Digital literacy is the new frontier of the opportunity gap.
  • Educating women improves child nutrition and reduces mortality rates.
2 Access to Healthcare

800 million women lack access to basic healthcare services.

  • Gender bias often leads to misdiagnosis of chronic pain and heart disease.
  • Maternal mortality remains high in low-income, rural regions.
3 Mental Health

Women are twice as likely to be diagnosed with depression or anxiety.

  • Postpartum depression affects 1 in 7 new mothers.
  • Societal stigma often prevents women from seeking help.

Education and Opportunities

Physical, sexual, and psychological violence remains one of the most widespread human rights violations globally.

1 The Literacy Gap

Two-thirds of the world's 773 million illiterate adults are women, a proportion that has remained virtually unchanged for two decades.

  • Female illiteracy is concentrated in sub-Saharan Africa, South Asia, and parts of the Middle East, where cultural norms often prioritize boys' education.
  • Illiteracy creates a cascading cycle of disadvantage: women who cannot read earn less, have poorer health outcomes, and are less able to advocate for themselves and their children.
  • Adult literacy programs designed specifically for women have shown transformative effects on entire communities, improving child nutrition, reducing infant mortality, and increasing household income.
  • Digital literacy is an emerging frontier: women are 20% less likely than men to use the internet, further widening the information and opportunity gap.
2 Dropout Rate Among Girls

An estimated 129 million girls are out of school globally, with adolescent girls facing the highest risk of never returning.

  • Early marriage is one of the leading causes of girls dropping out: 12 million girls are married before age 18 every year, effectively ending their education.
  • Lack of sanitation facilities, particularly menstrual hygiene resources, causes girls in many developing countries to miss school regularly or leave permanently.
  • In conflict zones, girls are 2.5 times more likely to be out of school than boys, as schools become targets and safety concerns mount.
  • Investing in girls' education yields enormous returns: each additional year of schooling can increase a girl's future earnings by up to 25%.
3 Acess to STEM & Vocational Training

Women represent only 28% of the global STEM workforce, and the gap widens significantly in fields like engineering and computer science.

  • Gender stereotypes and biases in the classroom discourage girls from pursuing math and science from an early age, creating a pipeline problem.
  • Women in STEM face hostile academic environments, fewer research funding opportunities, and higher attrition rates than their male counterparts.
  • Vocational and technical training programs often lack gender-sensitive design, failing to address barriers like childcare, safety, and flexible scheduling that women disproportionately face.
  • Countries investing in women's STEM education see measurable gains in innovation, economic growth, and the development of solutions that better serve diverse populations.

Social & Cultural Norms

Deep-rooted stereotypes and cultural practices continue to dictate women's roles, limit their freedoms, and perpetuate inequality across generations.

1 Gender Stereotypes

The belief that 'a woman's place is in the home' persists in many societies, restricting women's access to economic opportunities and public life.

  • Gender stereotypes are reinforced through media representation, educational materials, religious institutions, and family structures from early childhood.
  • Women perform an estimated 75% of the world's unpaid care and domestic work, limiting their time for paid employment, education, and civic participation.
  • Media portrayal of women as primarily caregivers, objects of beauty, or supporting characters reinforces limited expectations and aspirations for girls.
  • Challenging stereotypes through inclusive education, diverse media representation, and policy changes has shown measurable positive impacts on gender equality indicators.
2 Early & Child Marriage

Every year, 12 million girls are married before the age of 18, robbing them of their childhood, health, and future potential.

  • Child marriage is driven by poverty, gender inequality, traditions, and insecurity; families may see marrying young daughters as a way to reduce economic burden.
  • Girls who marry young are more likely to experience domestic violence, have complicated pregnancies, and contract HIV and other sexually transmitted infections.
  • Child brides are six times less likely to complete secondary school compared to their unmarried peers, perpetuating cycles of poverty and dependence.
  • While rates are declining globally, population growth means the absolute number of child brides remains high, requiring accelerated intervention.
3 Gender-Based Expectations

Rigid gender norms dictate behavior, career choices, and life paths, limiting individual freedom for both women and men.

  • Honor-based violence, including so-called 'honor killings,' is perpetuated by cultural expectations around women's sexual behavior and family reputation.
  • Women who defy cultural norms in career choices, dress, relationships, or lifestyle face social ostracism, family rejection, and sometimes legal consequences.
  • Boys raised in environments with rigid gender norms are more likely to perpetuate violence and discrimination against women as adults.
  • Community-led programs that engage men and boys in challenging harmful norms have shown promise in shifting attitudes and reducing gender-based violence.

Legal Rights

Despite progress in legislation, gaps in enforcement, discriminatory laws, and barriers to justice leave millions of women without adequate legal protection.

1 Protective Legislation

While 155 countries have at least one law on domestic violence, enforcement remains weak, and 49 countries have no such legislation at all.

  • Laws addressing sexual harassment in the workplace exist in only about 60% of countries, and many lack clear definitions, penalties, or reporting mechanisms.
  • Anti-dowry laws in countries like India exist on paper but are poorly enforced, with dowry-related violence and deaths continuing at alarming rates.
  • Labor laws often fail to protect women in informal employment, which accounts for over 60% of women's work in developing countries.
  • Effective legislation requires not just enactment but adequate funding for enforcement agencies, judiciary training, and victim support infrastructure.
2 Property & Inheritance Rights

In over 40% of countries, women do not have equal land and property rights, limiting their economic independence and security.

  • Customary and religious laws in many regions override national legislation, effectively denying women the right to inherit or own property.
  • Women own less than 20% of the world's land, despite being responsible for producing 60-80% of food in developing countries.
  • When women can own and control property, studies show increased household spending on nutrition, education, and healthcare for children.
  • Land titling programs that specifically include women's names have shown transformative effects on women's economic empowerment and bargaining power within households.
3 Access to Justice

Women face significant barriers in accessing legal systems, from unaffordable legal fees to gender-biased courts and social stigma.

  • In many countries, women's testimony is legally worth less than men's or is not accepted at all in certain types of cases.
  • Legal aid services are scarce and underfunded globally, with women in rural areas and indigenous communities facing the greatest access gaps.
  • Gender-based violence survivors face re-traumatization in court proceedings, lengthy delays, low conviction rates, and minimal sentences for perpetrators.
  • Investing in women's legal literacy, community paralegals, and gender-sensitive judicial training are proven strategies to improve women's access to justice.
